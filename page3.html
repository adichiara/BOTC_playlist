“Paranoia, paranoia, everybody’s coming to get me”—Harvey Danger kicks the door open with a line that could be the official motto of Blood on the Clocktower. "Flagpole Sitta" is an anthem for the anxious and unhinged, making it an almost too-perfect backdrop for a game where every glance feels like an accusation and every alliance could explode at any moment.

The song captures a mindset familiar to anyone who’s played Clocktower more than once: overthinking, second-guessing, spinning in circles trying to decide whether your gut is brilliant or just hungry. “I’m not sick but I’m not well” could just as easily describe the social tension at the table as it could a mental health check.

Its punchy alt-rock energy mirrors the game’s chaotic momentum—especially in the midgame, when nobody knows who to trust and every accusation feels both premature and necessary. And yet, beneath the sarcasm and self-deprecation, there’s real desperation: a desire to be seen, believed, vindicated.

That emotional volatility—ranging from defiance to collapse in under three minutes—is exactly what gives "Flagpole Sitta" its resonance. It's not just a fun, slightly unhinged pick; it’s a song that lives in the same emotional register as a Clocktower endgame meltdown.

#14 – "The End." by My Chemical Romance

 Win Rate: 87.07%

Listen on Spotify

“Now come one, come all to this tragic affair”—what better opening for a Blood on the Clocktower session than a theatrical invitation to doom?

"The End." is a curtain-raiser drenched in fatalism, cynicism, and flair—fitting perfectly with the game’s performative edge. From its baroque bombast to its existential dismay, this song doesn’t just set a mood; it casts a role. And that role is someone preparing for judgment, possibly death, probably both.

The lyric “You might wake up and notice you’re someone you’re not” lands with a particular kind of force here. In Clocktower, identity is never fixed—you're the Saint until you're the Spy. You're trusted until you're executed. And “The End.” embraces that shifting landscape with melodramatic glee.

Then there’s the eerie invitation to “save me,” a desperate plea echoed at so many Clocktower tables by players whose claims seem a little too neat, a little too rehearsed. The tension between sincerity and strategy lives in every beat of this track.

While "Dead!" (another MCR entry) leans more on wit and chaos, "The End." is pure dread in velvet robes. It feels like the emotional prologue to a doomed town square—a world where every player is just trying to survive their last monologue.

#13 – "Exit Music (For A Film)" by Radiohead

 Win Rate: 87.07%

Few songs summon a slow-building sense of dread like Radiohead’s “Exit Music (For A Film),” and few match its mood better than Blood on the Clocktower. The song opens in a hush—"Wake from your sleep"—and creeps forward like a secret spreading through town. It’s not just atmospheric; it’s prophetic. This is music for a final reckoning.

“Before all hell breaks loose,” Thom Yorke warns, and if that doesn’t describe the emotional cliff players stand on before the final vote, nothing does. The whispered tension in the verses mimics the game’s quiet discussions, while the explosive crescendo mirrors a town turning on itself in a frenzy of suspicion.

The lyric “We hope your rules and wisdom choke you” strikes especially hard—delivering a sharp stab to those who talk a little too confidently, who lean too heavily on logic while missing what’s right in front of them. It's a song for the player who knows something isn’t right but can’t prove it… yet.

With its haunting build, accusatory undertones, and beautifully restrained rage, “Exit Music” isn’t just a clever fit—it’s a chilling companion to the psychological unraveling that Clocktower does so well.

#12 – "The Pretender" by Foo Fighters

 Win Rate: 87.93%

With pounding drums and an unrelenting chorus, Foo Fighters’ “The Pretender” charges straight into the heart of Blood on the Clocktower—a game where performance is survival and the truth is constantly under siege. The track opens with a baited calm before erupting into full-blown resistance, echoing the way players hold their breath before accusations begin to fly.

“Keep you in the dark, you know they all pretend” isn’t just a lyric—it’s the meta. It describes the whole premise of Clocktower, where everyone is pretending to be someone they’re not, and pretending not to be someone they are. The song seethes with mistrust and challenge, as if daring someone to come clean… and knowing they won’t.

Then there’s the defiance: “What if I say I’m not like the others?” It’s a Villager calling out to a crowd that doesn’t believe them. It’s the Outsider doubting their worth. It’s every good-aligned player trying to make a case before being cast out anyway.

Its explosive tempo matches the frenzy of final rounds, where conversations double in speed and pressure mounts with each tick of the clock. A high win rate and a sharper message than most, “The Pretender” earns its spot as both an emotional release and a lyrical reflection of what makes this game so thrilling.

#11 – "The Place Where He Inserted the Blade" by Black Country, New Road

 Win Rate: 87.93%

Listen on Spotify

Few songs tap into the aching vulnerability beneath the surface of Blood on the Clocktower quite like this. “The Place Where He Inserted the Blade” is a slow-burn ballad of longing, betrayal, and emotional fragility—making it a uniquely poignant fit for a game built on trust that’s destined to fracture.

“You’re scared of a world where you’re needed,” the song begins, setting the tone for characters thrust into unexpected roles—Storytellers and Scapegoats, Saints and Serpents—each trying to matter in a world where they might not be believed. Its refrain, “Show me the place where he inserted the blade,” is a devastating mirror to the game’s inevitable turning point: when truth is weaponized, and the wound of betrayal is no longer theoretical.

The repeated line “I know you’re scared, well I’m scared too” captures the uneasy alliance between players. Even in competition, there’s a quiet camaraderie—a recognition that no one is entirely safe, and that the blade may already be in your back.

Musically subtle but emotionally gutting, this track isn’t just about suspicion or deceit—it’s about what it feels like to be lied to, believed, doubted, and undone. The perfect song for those quiet post-game moments, when the lies are over but the sting still lingers.
